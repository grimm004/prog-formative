Computational Automation

While computing has made a positive contribution in many areas of society, this webpage will focus on that of automation.

Computing has provided a means to digitally transform the way we go about doing trivial tasks as well as providing a way to reliably automate safety critical systems. However, some argue that mass automation takes away the need for many jobs, thus creating redundencies. The following will look at the advantages and disadvantages of computational automation.

Computation could be thought of as starting to automate strenuous parts of society as far back as the ninteenth century with concepts such as Babbage's Analytical Engine, a mechanical computer designed to reliably perform complex calculations. While this was never built, it proved testament to how mechanical systems could be used for computation.

In the post-industrial era, computer automation started to kick off. An early example of this was Alan Turing's electro-mechanical machine designed to find combinations to automate the cracking of the Enigma code, something that was previously not reasonably possible.

In addition to agricultural and factory automation to increase yield and production, and the use of the internet to automate specialist tasks accross the world from a single control room, other fields such as aviation have greatly benefited from the reliability provided by automation, these days planes practically fly themselves and pilots are just there to monitor systems, this has vastly reduced the number of instances of pilot error. Other safety-critical systems such as hospitals have also benefited from moving to electronic record storage for fast access.

Looking into the future there are plenty of exciting and promising automation technologies. These range from further manufacturing systems such as 3D printing to automatic diagnosis of illness to automated surgury (and other medical advancements). Many of these new technologies rely on advancements in other computation technologies such as machine learning and AI which makes it hard to predict how far out they are, however the future does seem exciting.

Computational automation however carries a constant concern of job loss as machines take over tasks traditionally done by hand. For example, pwc predicts that 3% of jobs are at risk of automation by the early 2020s. Additionally there are some instances where automation has faild, for example, a project launched in 2002 to automate record sharing in the NHS was eventually dismantled in 2011 after around £10bn of wasted taxpayer money. Even with this failure, successful instances of automation rely on advancements in cybersecurity technology to prevent unwanted control of systems (for example autopilot) or access to private data (for example patients' medical records).

In conclusion, computational automation has boosted global economies, improved safety and made some tasks easier to perform, however there is uncertainty about where it will take us in the future.
